Author

Mira Ellison

Mira Ellison writes speculative fiction at the intersection of contact linguistics and first-contact protocol. Drawing on a background in comparative literature and SETI-adjacent research, her work explores what it might actually mean to hear from—and talk back to—another civilization. She lives in the Pacific Northwest and is currently working on a series examining communication beyond the binary of language.

About Mira Ellison

Mira Ellison writes first contact science fiction in the epistolary tradition — fiction that unfolds through documents, records, and accumulated evidence rather than linear narrative. First Contact Diary presents the record of a first contact event through the accumulation of first-hand accounts, official documentation, and cross-referenced testimony — creating a picture that is more mosaic than narrative, more evidentiary than dramatic. Ellison’s approach inverts the typical first contact story structure: rather than building toward revelation, the diary format means the reader starts with the full weight of the event and works backward, assembling what happened from the fragments different witnesses provide. For readers who want science fiction that treats evidence seriously rather than as an obstacle to plot resolution, Mira Ellison is a distinctive new voice.

The epistolary form in science fiction is a reliable indicator of an author who is more interested in the social and institutional consequences of speculative premises than in the immediate physical drama. Ellison’s background in comparative literature is visible in the way the diary entries vary in voice, perspective, and reliability — different witnesses do not simply report the same event differently; they report importantly different subsets of the event, and the reader’s task is to triangulate the full picture from partial and sometimes contradictory accounts. This is a technique that requires significant craft to execute well, because the reader must always feel that the puzzle is solvable and that the author is not simply withholding information to manufacture suspense. Ellison’s first novel suggests she has that craft.

The SETI-adjacent research background is the other distinguishing element of Ellison’s approach. The current SETI (Search for Extraterrestrial Intelligence) discourse is predominantly about signal detection and radio frequency analysis — the engineering problem of finding evidence of non-human intelligence. Ellison is interested in the harder question: once you have found something, what then? What protocols exist for establishing communication with a intelligence that may not share your concept of communication? What happens to human institutions — governments, religions, scientific communities — when the question of non-human intelligence shifts from theoretical to confirmed? These are questions that SETI researchers themselves are beginning to ask seriously, and Ellison’s fiction brings the same rigor to them that the researchers bring to their engineering problems.
