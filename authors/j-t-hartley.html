About J. T. Hartley

Hartley's background is in environmental science and science journalism, with a career that has included fieldwork in glaciology, several years as a contributing editor at a science policy journal, and a long-running newsletter on climate adaptation that has accumulated a substantial readership among both specialists and general readers. Living with a Moving Planet emerged from a frustration with the framing of climate discourse — the tendency to treat climate change as a future crisis rather than a continuous condition, and to describe adaptation as a response to emergency rather than as a fundamental skill that humans have always needed and rarely practiced consciously.

The book works from deep time backward: it begins with what paleoclimatology tells us about how Earth's climate has shifted over the last hundred thousand years, uses that frame to establish that human civilization has always been a climate adaptation project, and then asks what it means to do that work deliberately rather than reactively. The source material — ice cores, sediment records, archaeological evidence of settlement patterns — is presented with enough detail to feel substantive but not enough to overwhelm the reader who came for the argument rather than the data. That balance is where Hartley operates best: the science serves the narrative, not the other way around.

For readers who have read widely in climate literature and found most of it either too technical or too alarming, Living with a Moving Planet offers something different: a calm, historically grounded case for treating climate adaptation as an intellectual discipline rather than a crisis response. Hartley's voice is methodical without being dry, and the hope in the book is the earned kind — not optimism about outcomes but confidence in human capacity, backed by evidence rather than sentiment.
