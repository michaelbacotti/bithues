Maris K. Vector

Maris K. Vector writes literary science fiction that prizes atmosphere and interiority over spectacle. With a background in optics and wave physics, they bring a physicist’s intuition to fiction — asking how light, probability, and observation shape the worlds we think we see. The Probability of Light is their debut novel.

About Maris K Vector

Maris K Vector writes science fiction that treats probability and light as narrative subjects rather than backdrop. The Probability of Light uses the physical behavior of light — its wave-particle duality, its interaction with probability amplitudes, its role in observation itself — as a metaphor and mechanism for character choices in situations where the outcome is genuinely uncertain. The audiobook edition suggests an author who thinks carefully about the relationship between text and voice, and who has made the narrative available in a format that emphasizes its lyrical and rhythmic dimensions. As a new release in April 2026, Maris K Vector is a new voice in science fiction whose specific combination of physics and narrative suggests a project worth following.

The author’s background in optics and wave physics is visible in the specific quality of attention the prose brings to visual and perceptual phenomena. This is not science fiction where the physics is backdrop — where the author knows just enough about quantum mechanics to invoke it as a magic system. Vector’s engagement with probability theory and wave mechanics reads as genuine and specific, which means the book’s science is something the reader can actually think about rather than merely accept. The best science fiction that uses physics as a thematic element is the fiction that makes the physics thinkable, and Vector has the background to make that happen in a way that authors who have only read about physics rather than done it cannot.

The literary science fiction classification is accurate and probably the most useful genre marker for readers navigating the current landscape of speculative fiction. Vector’s fiction does not prioritize plot velocity or action sequences; it prioritizes the interior experience of characters navigating situations where their understanding of reality is being challenged at the level of fundamental physics. The comparison that comes to mind is Greg Egan at his most accessible — science fiction where the ideas are the characters’ primary emotional reality — though Vector’s prose has a warmer quality than Egan’s typical register. For readers who want science fiction that rewards close attention to its conceptual framework, The Probability of Light is a debut that announces a voice with something original to say.
