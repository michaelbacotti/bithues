About D. E. Harlan

D. E. Harlan writes nonfiction at the intersection of cognitive science, philosophy of mind, and practical psychology. Two books — Beyond the Veil and Mindful Memory — represent a coherent project: what can we know about consciousness, and what can we do with that knowledge in everyday life? Beyond the Veil approaches death and near-death experiences through the lens of quantum physics and cross-cultural NDE research, building an argument for consciousness that resists both materialist reduction and new-age mysticism. Mindful Memory takes that framework and applies it to cognitive health in aging, grounding memory palaces and timeline mapping in neuroscience without losing the practical dimension. Harlan’s voice is rigorous but warm — the kind of writer who makes a reader feel like a serious topic is finally being treated seriously.

The two-book project has a clear arc: the first book builds the theoretical framework — what consciousness is, how it relates to the physical brain, what the NDE evidence suggests — and the second book applies that framework to a specific, high-stakes domain: the cognitive decline that aging brings. The transition from theory to application is handled with unusual care: Harlan does not claim more for the framework than it can deliver, but insists that even a partial understanding of consciousness’s nature can generate useful practical strategies for memory and cognitive maintenance.

Harlan’s avoidance of both the materialist reductionism of mainstream neuroscience and the uncritical mysticism of the popular consciousness literature is the book’s most distinctive feature. Both positions are easy: reductionism requires only the confidence that the physical brain is sufficient to explain everything, and mysticism requires only the willingness to believe that consciousness is inexplicable and therefore supernatural. Harlan occupies the harder middle ground — taking consciousness seriously as a phenomenon that does not fit neatly into either category, and building an argument that accounts for the evidence without retreating into comfortable oversimplification. For readers who have found both mainstream neuroscience and spiritual alternatives unsatisfying, this is a rare voice worth reading.
