About Luna Sage

Luna Sage writes practical self-help for readers who want structure without dogma. Shadow Work Journal for Women is a 90-day guided journal designed for emotional healing work — specifically the kind that involves tracing present-day emotional triggers to their formative origins and choosing new responses in their light. Sage's approach is psychologically current without being clinical: the prompts are warm, direct, and specific enough to generate genuine self-reflection rather than vague yes-feeling. The 90-day format is long enough to build real habit but not so long that momentum dissipates before completion. Unlike many shadow work books, this one does not assume prior familiarity with Jungian psychology — it builds the framework from the ground up through the prompts themselves. For women beginning emotional healing work, this is one of the most usable resources available in the genre.

Sage's background is in counseling psychology, with a private practice that specialized in trauma recovery and relational healing before she shifted to writing. The transition from clinical work to self-publishing reflects a conviction that the most useful psychological tools should not require a therapist’s office to access — that guided self-work at scale is only possible through books. The Shadow Work Journal emerged from years of seeing clients who needed a structure they could take home and use independently, and who were not well-served by the vague, mystical framing that shadow work often carries in popular culture. Sage’s explicit goal was to produce a shadow work resource that a clinical therapist could hand to a client with confidence that it would not introduce confusion or false self-diagnosis.

The darker elements in Sage’s work — the language of shadow, of facing what you have disowned — are not aesthetic choices but functional ones. Shadow work is difficult precisely because it involves encountering parts of yourself that you have learned to push away, and the vocabulary of darkness serves a real purpose: it names the territory without dressing it up. For readers who have tried journaling practices that felt sanitized and unhelpful, the directness of this approach will feel like a relief.
