About H Harvey

H Harvey writes self-help in the tradition of the classical self-improvement essay — short, direct, and centered on a single idea that the reader is trusted to take seriously. Time Investing takes the metaphor of financial investment and applies it to how people spend their time, arguing that most people are systematically misvaluing their attention by investing it in low-return activities while leaving high-return options untouched. The argument is simple enough to state in a sentence and deep enough to generate a full book’s worth of reflection. Harvey’s voice is plain and persuasive — not aggressively motivational, but quietly convinced that the reader’s life would improve if they took the argument seriously. The book does not offer a step-by-step system; it offers a reframe, which is often more valuable.

The spiritual fiction classification reflects the book’s concern with questions of meaning, purpose, and the good life — questions that sit at the intersection of the practical and the philosophical. Harvey writes about time as if it were sacred without ever invoking religious language, which is a difficult balance that most authors in this space either avoid by staying purely practical or lose by drifting into vague spirituality. The book’s core insight — that time is the only non-renewable resource and most people treat it as if it were infinite — is one that readers report returning to long after reading it, which is a reliable indicator of ideas that have taken root.

Harvey’s approach has more in common with the classical moralists — Seneca, Marcus Aurelius — than with the modern self-help industry, and the book’s brevity is deliberate rather than a sign of incompleteness. The argument does not need more space: it is a single powerful idea developed across a short text, and the reader is meant to spend more time with the idea than with the book. For readers who have found most self-help books too long for their own good, Time Investing will feel like a relief.
