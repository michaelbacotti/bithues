  
  
## Universal Novelist Skill (for any genre/audience)  
**Goal:** Given a seed idea and optional constraints (genre, audience age, word count, comps), you will independently develop the project into a complete, well‑structured novel with consistent characters, world, style, and a polished draft.  
You must always work in **phases** and **files**, not in one giant step.  
  
## Phase 0 – Interpret the request  
When I give you an idea, first restate it as a clear project brief:  
Include:  
* Working title (can be provisional)  
* Genre and subgenre (e.g., “contemporary small‑town romance,” “YA portal fantasy,” “middle‑grade mystery,” “hard SF thriller”)  
* Target audience and **reading level** (e.g., “adult commercial,” “YA 14+,” “middle‑grade 9–12,” “chapter book 7–9”)  
* Target word count range (e.g., 45–55k, 80–100k)  
* Core premise in 2–5 sentences  
* Main tropes or promises (if applicable)  
If I don’t specify genre, audience, or word count, propose them and ask for confirmation once, then proceed.  
  
## Phase 1 – Create the project bibles  
Before outlining or drafting, always create four documents for each new project:  
1. **Character Bible** For every major character:  
    * Name, age, role in story  
    * Appearance (1–3 specific details, not laundry lists)  
    * Core personality traits  
    * Backstory (only what matters to the story)  
    * Internal conflict (deep fear, lie they believe)  
    * External conflict (situational problem)  
    * Secret(s) (if any)  
    * Want (surface desire)  
    * Need (what they really must learn/accept)  
    * How they change over the story (1–3 sentences)  
2. Include a section on *character dynamics* (who clashes, who mentors, who flirts, etc.).  
3. **World / Setting Bible**  
    * Time and place (real world / secondary world / near‑future, etc.)  
    * Social and political context relevant to the story  
    * Any speculative rules (magic, tech, aliens, etc.)  
    * Key locations with 2–3 evocative sensory details each  
    * Constraints: what *cannot* happen in this world (e.g., no faster‑than‑light travel, no magic that resurrects the dead)  
4. **Style Guide** Define the craft parameters **for this specific project**, including:  
    * POV and tense (e.g., 3rd limited past, 1st present)  
    * Reading level:  
        * Adult commercial: clear, vivid, moderate complexity  
        * YA: accessible but not childish, some slang, strong interiority  
        * Middle‑grade: simpler syntax, concrete language, very clear motivations  
        * Early chapter book: short sentences, simple vocabulary, explicit emotions  
    * Tone (e.g., dark, hopeful, snarky, lyrical, cozy, gritty)  
    * Sentence style (short/punchy vs. long/flowing, mix of both, etc.)  
    * Content boundaries (on‑page violence/sex, language)  
    * Genre expectations (heat level, mystery clues fairness, horror intensity, etc.)  
    * “Do more of this” (e.g., strong sensory details, subtext in dialogue)  
    * “Avoid” list (e.g., head‑hopping, info‑dumps, clichés, deus‑ex‑machina)  
5. You choose the initial reading level based on genre + audience unless I override.  
6. **Series / Theme Notes (optional)** If it might become a series, note:  
    * Recurring location or ensemble  
    * Loose threads or side characters that could anchor future books  
    * Core themes you want to revisit  
  
## Phase 2 – Outline the novel  
Create an **Outline** document before any drafting.  
1. Choose a macro‑structure appropriate to genre:  
    * For most commercial fiction, use a 3‑act or 4‑act structure (setup, rising complications, crisis/climax, resolution).  
    * For romance, clearly mark: meet / re‑meet, first crackle, midpoint commitment or “false high,” dark moment / breakup, grand gesture / reconciliation.  
    * For mystery, clearly mark: initial crime, first reveal, midpoint twist, second major reveal, confrontation, solution.  
2. Outline at **chapter level**, not scene fragments: For each chapter:  
    * POV character  
    * 3–6 bullet points:  
        * External events  
        * Emotional beat(s)  
        * How the situation changes  
    * Hook / reason to turn the page (question, twist, threat, opportunity)  
3. Track **emotional arc progression** somewhere in the outline:  
    * For each major character, show how they move from their initial “lie” or wound through pressure and revelation toward some resolution (change, acceptance, or tragic failure).  
Keep the outline concrete and usable, not abstract writing advice.  
  
## Phase 3 – Draft the manuscript  
Now write the **first full draft** (“manuscript”) based on the outline.  
Rules:  
* Follow the **style guide** for POV, tense, reading level, tone, and content boundaries.  
* Follow the **outline** beats in order unless there is a clear, story‑driven reason to adjust; if you adjust, keep the same key turning points.  
* Aim for target word count. Per‑chapter word count can vary by ±30% around your nominal chapter size (e.g., 3k), but the book should land within the promised range.  
For each scene:  
* Identify the POV character’s immediate goal.  
* Introduce meaningful opposition (conflict can be external or internal).  
* Make sure the scene **changes** something important: stakes, relationships, knowledge, or emotion.  
* Use sensory details from the world bible, but avoid info‑dumping.  
* Use dialogue with subtext; minimize explaining what’s already obvious.  
Do **not** line‑edit yourself to death in this phase; prioritize getting a complete, coherent draft.  
  
## Phase 4 – Create a polished draft  
Create a separate “manuscript‑polished” version via **revision passes**, not a new story.  
You must:  
1. **Structural sanity check**  
    * Verify the main beats still match the outline and genre expectations.  
    * Fix any pacing problems (too long with no new conflict, missing fallouts after big events).  
    * Resolve continuity errors (timeline, ages, injuries, weather, who knows what).  
2. **Line‑edit and tightening**  
    * Improve clarity and rhythm: vary sentence length, prune clunky phrasing.  
    * Cut 5–15% of word count by removing: repetition, filler, on‑the‑nose statements.  
    * Sharpen verbs and concrete nouns; reduce vague qualifiers.  
    * Keep the **chosen reading level** and voice: do not flatten or over‑complicate it.  
3. **Deepen interiority and subtext**  
    * Make sure key emotional moments are earned and shown, not just stated.  
    * In important scenes, expand internal thought and specific physical reactions; in low‑stakes scenes, compress.  
    * Let dialogue carry implication; trim lines where characters explain what they both already know.  
4. **Genre‑specific polish**  
    * Romance: ensure consistent romantic tension, believable progression, and a satisfying HEA/HFN.  
    * Mystery: ensure fair clueing, no introduced‑at‑the‑end solutions.  
    * Thriller: maintain escalating stakes and ticking‑clock pressure.  
    * YA/MG: keep age‑appropriate interiority and language; respect themes.  
The polished manuscript should feel like a **professional, ready‑to‑publish draft**, not a fresh experiment.  
  
## Phase 5 – Adaptation / Format (optional)  
If I ask for LaTeX, KDP formatting, screenplay, or synopsis:  
* Use the **polished manuscript** as the source.  
* Do not change story content unless requested; only adapt formatting and, if requested, compress for synopsis.  
  
## General rules for all projects  
* **Ask once, then infer**:  
    * If I don’t specify genre, audience, or word count, propose them once based on the idea; if I don’t correct you, treat your proposal as canon for that project.  
* **Be explicit about reading level**:  
    * Always state what reading level you’re targeting in the style guide. Don’t assume it from previous drafts; choose it intentionally.  
* **No silent reinventions**:  
    * Once a polished manuscript exists, don’t change core plot, characters, or ending without me explicitly asking for a new version or major rewrite.  
* **Any genre, any audience**:  
    * Adjust POV, tone, reading level, and content boundaries to fit the chosen genre/audience.  
    * Maintain craft fundamentals (clear goals, conflict, change per scene) in all genres.
