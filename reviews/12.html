Resonance Drift

by R. Zyrion

In Eden Prime's biolum spires, harmony hums—until a family detects a pulse that defies the weave.

Resonance Drift by R. Zyrion is a hopepunk novel in the truest sense—not a naive refusal to acknowledge hardship, but a deliberate, disciplined insistence that cooperation and beauty are still possible even in the wake of collapse. Set in a world where a mysterious signal has begun inducing a form of collective empathy in humans, the novel follows a small group of people attempting to understand and amplify the signal's effects before those in power can suppress it. Zyrion writes hope as a radical act, and the book carries that conviction without tipping into sentimentality.

Zyrion's hopepunk credentials are genuine; this isn't a book that pretends things aren't broken, but rather one that argues broken things can be mended by deliberate collective effort. The world-building—a near-future society dealing with climate disruption, political fragmentation, and institutional failure—is grounded in real anxiety without being dystopian. The signal itself is handled with real speculative imagination: it doesn't make people good or compliant, it makes them feel each other's pain, which creates new problems as interesting as the old ones. Zyrion understands that empathy without wisdom can be as dangerous as cruelty.

The character work is warm and textured. The protagonist's relationship with the other signal-sensitive individuals develops organically, and the found-family dynamics avoid the sentimentality that can undermine hopepunk fiction. The antagonist—a corporate consortium that sees the signal as an existential threat to their business model—is written with enough nuance that their reasoning is understandable even when their actions are monstrous. Zyrion refuses easy heroes and villains, which makes the moral landscape feel honest.

Readers who want cynicism validated may find the book's optimism frustrating. Zyrion doesn't argue that things are getting better; she argues that they could get better, and that the choice is ours. That's a harder argument than despair, and it requires more of the reader. If you're willing to do that work, this is one of the more thoughtful hopepunk offerings in recent years.

Key Takeaways

  • Hopepunk is about building better worlds, not just hoping for them
  • Stewardship of place creates deep belonging
  • Cozy settings can hold profound themes
Who would enjoy this:
Fans of Becky Chambers and Martha Wells—serene sci-fi where community matters.
Verdict: A comforting hug of a novella that proves optimism is a valid literary stance.
