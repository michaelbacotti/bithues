Bithues Reading Lab — Grief & Loss

Best Books About Grief — Navigate Loss with Wisdom

Last updated: April 2026

Home › Books › Grief & Loss

Grief is not a problem to solve. It's a passage to walk — and the right books can remind you that you're not alone, that what you're feeling is human, and that it's possible to integrate loss into your life without being destroyed by it. These are the books that do that best.

The Other Side of Sadness by George Bonanno

Bonanno, a clinical psychologist at Columbia, challenges the conventional picture of grief as a linear process with predictable stages. His research shows that most people are more resilient than the "stages of grief" model suggests — grief often comes in waves rather than stages, and many people report post-traumatic growth years after loss. This is grief science, not grief mythology.

Buy The Other Side of Sadness on Amazon →

The Year of Magical Thinking by Joan Didion

Didion's husband died suddenly on the day their daughter was in a coma — and this memoir tracks the year that followed. It is written with the exact precision and restraint that characterized all of Didion's work, and that restraint makes it more devastating, not less. She writes about grief not as catharsis but as a sustained encounter with reality that doesn't let you off the hook.

Buy The Year of Magical Thinking on Amazon →

Option B by Sheryl Sandberg and Adam Grant

Sandberg lost her husband in 2015 and Grant, a Wharton psychologist, helped her process it. Together they wrote a book about building resilience in the face of adversity — drawing on both personal narrative and organizational psychology research. It's warmer and more practical than most grief books, and particularly useful for people who need concrete frameworks for rebuilding.

Buy Option B on Amazon →

It's OK That You're Not OK by Megan Devine

Devine lost her husband to drowning in 2005 and found that most grief resources either minimized the pain or offered false promises of resolution. This book is her response — a clear-eyed argument that grief doesn't need to be fixed or transformed into something positive. Sometimes the most honest thing you can do is sit with the pain. Especially useful for people who feel broken by grief rather than supported.

Buy It's OK That You're Not OK on Amazon →

When Breath Becomes Air by Paul Kalanithi

Kalanithi was a neurosurgeon diagnosed with stage IV lung cancer at thirty-six. He spent his final fourteen months writing a memoir about facing mortality — not as an abstract philosophical exercise but as someone who understood exactly what was happening in his brain as he died. It is about grief in the most direct sense: a man losing his own life, and making meaning of it on the way.

Buy When Breath Becomes Air on Amazon →

The Unquiet by Linda Pastan

Pastan's poetry collection about her husband's death is among the finest grief literature in any form. Each poem takes a small, specific moment — a piece of clothing, a grocery list, a chair — and makes it carry the weight of loss without sentimentalizing it. You can read it in an afternoon and return to it for years.

Buy The Unquiet on Amazon →

Being with Dying by Joan Halifax

Halifax has worked with dying people for decades as a Buddhist teacher and hospice advocate. This book offers a framework for facing death — yours or someone else's — with compassion and without denial. It's calm, experienced, and practical, written by someone who has sat with thousands of people at the edge of life and found courage there, not despair.

Buy Being with Dying on Amazon →

First Ad Signs of Fading by Jonathan Robinson

Robinson is a psychotherapist who spent years working with grief and loss before experiencing the death of his own father. This short book is part memoir, part therapeutic guide — honest about how professional expertise doesn't protect you from the raw experience of loss, and how learning to be present with grief is a skill that deepens with practice.

Buy First Ad Signs of Fading on Amazon →

A Monster Calls by Patrick Ness

A young adult novel about a boy whose mother is dying of cancer. The monster who visits Conor at night forces him to tell the truth about how he's feeling — not the comfortable truth, the real one. It is about grief for children, but adults have found it one of the most honest portrayals of what grief actually feels like, not what we think it should feel like.

Buy A Monster Calls on Amazon →

💡 Key Takeaway

If you're in active grief right now, start with It's OK That You're Not OK — it gives you permission to feel what you're feeling without asking you to be positive about it. If you lost someone recently and want to understand the research on grief resilience, try The Other Side of Sadness. If you want to read something beautiful and true, read When Breath Becomes Air.
