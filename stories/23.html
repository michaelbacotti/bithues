The Ember Song

3,600 words · 12 min read

Before the world was, there was fire.

Not physical fire — that came later. But the concept of fire. The first spark of something from nothing. The energy that would eventually become everything.

And the First Ones sang to it.

***

Lyra was the last of the Chanters. A dying breed in a world that had forgotten magic. She lived in a cottage at the edge of the world — or so it seemed — tending a fire that had burned since before humans learned to speak.

Her grandmother had taught her the songs. Not through words, but through memory. The Chants weren't learned — they were inherited, passed down through blood and bone and the deepest parts of the soul.

But now the songs were dying. Each generation found fewer children with the gift. And Lyra, at ninety-three years old, was the last one who could carry the old tunes.

***

The young man arrived at midwinter. His name was Tobias, and he had walked for three months to reach her cottage. He was sick — fading, the doctors called it — and he had heard rumors of the Chanters who could heal through song.

"I'm not a healer," Lyra told him. "The songs aren't medicine."

"Then what are they?"

She thought for a long moment. "Memory. The songs remember what the world was. What it could be again. They don't change things — they remind them what they're supposed to be."

***

Tobias stayed. Days turned to weeks, weeks to months. He couldn't learn the songs — the gift didn't work that way — but he could listen. And slowly, something unexpected happened.

He started to remember too.

Not the songs themselves, but the feeling behind them. The sense that the world was more than it appeared. That there was magic in the mundane, if you knew how to look.

"I can feel it," he told Lyra one evening. "The ember. It's still there, underneath everything. Waiting."

Lyra smiled. This was the gift she could give. Not the songs themselves — those would die with her — but the understanding. The knowledge that magic was real, even if no one could wield it anymore.

Because belief is its own kind of magic.

***

Lyra died in the spring, the fire going out for the first time in ten thousand years. But Tobias carried something with him when he left — not the songs, but the memory of them.

He told others. Wrote books. Spoke of what he'd heard, what he'd felt. And though the world didn't believe him, some part of them remembered.

That's how it works, sometimes. Magic doesn't disappear. It just goes quiet, waiting for someone to listen.

And one day, someone will. Someone with the gift. Someone who can sing the First Songs again, and remind the world what it forgot.

Until then, the ember waits.
