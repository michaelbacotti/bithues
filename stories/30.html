American Voices

2,400 words · 8 min read

Languages don't just carry words. They carry worlds.

That's what Maria's grandmother always said. She had come to America from Guatemala when she was young, and she had spent her whole life living between two languages — two cultures, two ways of seeing.

Maria grew up bilingual, but she never really understood what that meant until she started teaching.

***

Her students came from everywhere. Vietnam. Somalia. Poland. Mexico. Each one carried a language in their head that their new country didn't understand.

They struggled with English — the grammar, the sounds, the idioms that made no sense. But they also struggled with their own languages, the ones they were forgetting. The words that used to come easily now required effort.

"I feel like I'm losing myself," one student told her. "I can't speak my language anymore, but I don't feel American either. I don't feel like anything."

***

Maria started a project. She asked her students to write in both languages — English and their native tongue. She didn't care if it was grammatically correct. She cared about the voice.

What emerged was beautiful. Stories about leaving home. About building new lives. About the things that got lost in translation — and the things that couldn't be translated at all.

A Polish student wrote about her grandmother's recipes, the ones that tasted like childhood. A Somali student wrote about the ocean crossing, the fear and hope that accompanied it. A Vietnamese student wrote about his father's shop, the smell of incense and fresh bread.

***

They were all American stories. Not despite being in other languages, but because of it. America wasn't a single language or culture. It was a collection of voices, each one adding to the chorus.

Maria put together a collection — a book of student writing, printed and bound. At the launch, parents and grandparents came, some speaking little English, but all understanding the same thing:

Language isn't just communication. It's connection. It's identity. It's the voice you carry with you, wherever you go.

***

Maria's grandmother passed away a few years later. At her funeral, Maria spoke in both English and Spanish — because her grandmother had lived in both worlds, and she deserved to be remembered in both.

That's what it means to be American, Maria realized. Not speaking one language. But carrying many. Holding multiple worlds in your head and heart. Letting them inform each other, challenge each other, enrich each other.

That's not confusion. That's multiplicity. That's the American voice.

And it's beautiful.
