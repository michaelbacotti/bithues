Builds a Robot

1,500 words · 5 min read

Jake had a big dream.

He wanted to build a robot. Not a toy robot from a store — a real robot. One that could walk and talk and do things. One that could be his friend.

He started collecting parts from around the house. Old boxes, broken toys, wires and wheels and things that used to belong to his dad.

"What are you making?" his mom asked.

"A friend," Jake said.

***

The robot took a long time to build. Jake worked on it every day after school. He used tape and glue and lots of imagination. He gave it a body made of cardboard, arms made of pipes, and eyes made of old flashlights.

It didn't look like the robots in the movies. It looked a little messy. A little strange. But Jake loved it.

"I need to teach you how to move," he told his robot. And so he did — using strings and wheels and pivots he learned about in his science class.

***

The robot couldn't really talk. But Jake pretended it could. He made up conversations, giving the robot a funny voice that made them both laugh.

He taught the robot to wave. To dance. To say "hello" in a squeaky voice.

It wasn't the smartest robot in the world. But it was his. And that made it perfect.

***

Jake's dad helped him enter the robot in the school science fair. There were other robots there — bigger ones, fancier ones, ones that could do amazing things.

Jake's robot wasn't the best. But it was the most creative. The judges loved how he had made it from things he found around the house. They gave him a prize for "Most Imaginative Invention."

Jake was proud. But more importantly, he had learned something valuable:

You don't need fancy parts to build something amazing. You need imagination. And patience. And the willingness to try, even when things don't work out.

That's how you build a robot. That's how you build anything.
