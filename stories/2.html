The Last Winter

3,800 words · 13 min read

The snow began to fall on the day Uluk turned sixteen, and by the third day, it had buried the world.

Koda stood at the mouth of the cave and watched the white void consume the valley. The sky was the color of old bone. The wind carried a sound that wasn't quite a sound — a silence so deep it felt like pressure against his ears.

"They won't come back," Uluk said behind him.

Koda didn't turn. He knew. He'd known since the second day, when the hunting party hadn't returned and the tracks they'd left behind had filled with snow so quickly it was as if the earth wanted to erase them.

The elders said the winter was punishment. The sky had turned against them because someone had broken the old laws. But Koda's father — Tal, the best tracker the tribe had ever known — had left with six hunters three days ago, and nothing in the world could punish a man more than leaving his son alone in a winter that might never end.

"We have enough food for six days," Uluk said. He was trying to sound calm, but Koda heard the crack in his voice. Uluk had never led anything. He was the healer's apprentice, not a hunter. But the old chief was dead, killed in the rockslide that buried the southern storage, and the only other men were the elders who could barely walk. "If we ration."

"Six days." Koda finally turned. Uluk looked smaller than he'd ever looked — shoulders hunched, hands trembling. Not from cold. From fear. "And then?"

Uluk didn't answer. They both knew what came after six days.

The tribe had seventeen people left. Twelve children, three elders, and two young men who should have been hunters but had never been tested. Koda had brought down his first deer at twelve, but he'd never tracked in a white world where the prey could see you before you saw them.

"I'll go," Koda said.

"You'll die."

"Maybe." Koda pulled his father's fur cloak tighter. It still smelled like smoke and elk and the particular scent that was just Tal — now fading. "But if I don't, we all die anyway. At least this way, there's a chance."

Uluk grabbed his arm. "That's not what a leader would say."

"I'm not a leader."

"The tribe needs a leader."

Koda looked at Uluk — the healer's apprentice, the boy who'd never held a spear, who trembled in the face of winter but stood anyway. "Then you lead. Keep them together. Keep them alive. I'll bring back food or I'll die trying."

He pulled away and walked into the white.

The world swallowed him whole.

***

Time became strange in the snow. Koda walked for what felt like hours, though the sun never moved — just a pale disk behind the gray, marking noon without providing light. His legs burned. His face went numb. He kept one hand on the cliff wall and walked east, because his father had always said that the deer moved east when the snow came, following the valleys down to where the cold couldn't reach.

He found the first body after what might have been half a day.

Renn. One of the hunters. He was frozen against a rock, one hand still reaching for something — a root, maybe, buried under the white. His eyes were open, glazed with ice. Koda stopped. He didn't have the strength to bury him. He didn't have the time.

He kept walking.

The second hunter was farther on, and the third. Each one a man Koda had known — had eaten with, had learned from, had watched teach their children to throw spears. He couldn't bury them either. The best he could do was close their eyes and whisper the words the elders taught: Go to the fire that burns in the mountain. Go to the place where the ancestors wait. You are not alone.

By the fourth body, he understood. They'd been heading the same direction. Following the same path. And something had caught them — not the cold, not the snow. Something that waited in the white.

He kept walking anyway. What else could he do?

***

The deer appeared like a ghost.

One moment, Koda was alone in the void. The next, there it was — a doe, standing on a ridge above him, watching with eyes that held no fear. It should have run. They always ran. But this one just watched, head tilted, as if considering something.

Koda raised his spear. His arm was shaking so badly he could barely hold it.

The deer didn't move.

"Go," he whispered. "Run. I can't — I don't want to —"

But his body was moving anyway, driven by something older than want. Survival. The thing that made you kill even when you were too tired to lift your arms.

He threw.

The spear flew wide. Of course it did. He was done, finished, his body had nothing left —

The deer walked into the path of the spear. It didn't flinch. It just... walked into it, as if it had planned this. As if it wanted this.

The sound it made wasn't a sound. It was a feeling — a resonance, deep in Koda's chest, like a drumbeat from somewhere far away. Then the deer fell, and the snow around it turned red, and Koda was running again, driven by the sudden need to get there before it got away.

He didn't question it. He was too tired to question anything.

***

By the time he拖 the deer back to the cave — it took three times as long as the walk out, dragging the body through the snow — the moon had risen and set twice. He didn't remember most of it. Fragments: the taste of ice, the burn of his muscles, the sound of something following him that was never quite there.

But he made it.

Uluk was waiting at the cave mouth. His face went white when he saw the deer — then red, then something that might have been tears freezing on his cheeks.

"Koda."

"We need to eat," Koda said. His voice didn't sound like his own. "All of us. Now."

***

The meat lasted nineteen days. By the end, they'd eaten everything — even the bones, cracked for marrow. But the snow had stopped, and on the twentieth day, the elders went out and came back with roots they'd stored in the old way, buried in places only they remembered.

The tribe would live.

Koda found Uluk alone in the back of the cave, staring at the wall.

"You saved us," Uluk said without turning.

"I brought back a deer."

"You walked into the white when everyone else was too scared. You walked into the white and you came back."

Koda sat down beside him. His body still hurt. It would hurt for months. But he was alive, and everyone was alive, and that was what mattered.

"I saw something," Koda said. "In the white. The deer — it let me kill it. It walked into the spear."

Uluk was quiet for a long time. "The old ones believed the world gives and takes. Maybe it was time for the world to give."

"Maybe." Koda looked at his hands. They were scarred now, fingers that would never work quite right. "Or maybe I just got lucky."

"Maybe both."

They sat in the dark, two boys who should have been hunters, who'd become something else in the white. Outside, the world was turning again — the snow melting, the world returning. Somewhere, a bird sang.

"Uluk."

"Yeah?"

"I'm not a leader."

"I know."

"But I'll help. Whatever you need."

Uluk turned. For a moment, he looked like what he was — a young man, scared, thrust into something too big for him. Then something shifted. Something older.

"That's what a leader does," he said. "Finds the right people and keeps them together."

He held out his hand.

Koda took it.

***

They found the cave paintings a generation later — Koda's grandson leading the expedition, Uluk's great-granddaughter at his side. The images showed a boy walking into the white, a deer that walked into a spear, a fire that kept the darkness away. And at the very end, two hands reaching for each other.

The elders said it was a story about the old days. About survival. About what it meant to be human.

They were right, of course. But they were also wrong. It wasn't about survival. It was about what you survived for.

Each other. That was the only thing worth surviving for.
