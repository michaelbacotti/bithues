Blood Ties

3,700 words · 13 min read

The first night I killed someone, I was sixteen years old.

Not human. I wasn't that far gone — not yet. But the woman in the alley looked at me like I was prey, and I proved her right.

My name is Marcus, and I'm a vampire hunter. Or at least, that's what I tell myself. The truth is more complicated.

***

My mother was one of them. That's how it works — the gift, the curse, whatever you want to call it. It passes through blood. One bite, and you're changed. Most don't survive the change. Those who do become something else.

She did. And she made me.

I've spent fifteen years trying to understand why she turned me. Was it love? Was it hunger? Was it something else entirely — a need to create something that would outlast her?

I'll never know. She's been dead for a decade now, and the answers died with her.

***

The hunting was supposed to be penance. For the things I've done. For the things I am. Every vampire I kill is one more crime I don't commit. Every monster I destroy is proof that I'm not one of them.

Except I am. That's the joke. The cosmic irony that keeps me awake at night.

I hunt my own kind. Not because I have to. But because it's the only way I know how to live with myself.

***

Her name was Elena. Twenty-three, freshly turned, terrified out of her mind. She found me because she had nowhere else to go — the vampire community doesn't welcome newcomers with open arms. They use them, abuse them, and discard them when they're done.

"I didn't ask for this," she said, tears streaming down her face. "I was walking home from work. She just... appeared. And then..."

I knew the story. I'd heard it a hundred times. The difference was, most of those who told it didn't survive long enough to become vampires.

"I can help you," I said. "Or I can end it. Your choice."

She looked at me with those red-rimmed eyes — not red from bloodlust yet, but soon — and asked the question they all ask:

"Does it get better?"

***

I've been doing this for fifteen years. I've killed more vampires than I can count. I've saved dozens of newly turned, shown them how to live with what they've become.

But I've never answered that question honestly. Because the truth is: it doesn't get better. It just gets different. You learn to manage the hunger. You learn to live with the things you've done. You learn to see the world in a new way, colored by blood and shadow.

It doesn't get better. But it does get easier.

***

Elena stayed with me. Not as a partner — though there were moments, late at night, when we both wondered — but as something more like family. Two vampires, bound by blood and circumstance, trying to find our way in a world that didn't want us.

We started taking in others. The newly turned. The desperate. The ones who had no one else. We taught them what we knew. How to hunt without killing. How to live with what they were. How to find meaning in an existence that had no meaning.

We called ourselves the Blood Keepers. A support group for monsters who wanted to be something more.

***

The vampires who run the city didn't like it. We were disrupting their feed. Their control. Their carefully balanced ecosystem of predator and prey.

So they came for us.

It was a massacre — their word, not mine. But we survived. Some of us, anyway. The ones who mattered. The ones who understood that survival isn't about living forever.

It's about living for something.

***

Now I hunt less and teach more. The younger vampires, the ones just turned, they need guidance. They need to know that they don't have to become monsters. They can choose to be something else.

Elena runs the house now. We've got seventeen members — all ages, all backgrounds, all united by the curse that binds us. We're not a family, exactly. But we're something close.

Does it get better?

I still don't know. But I know this: it gets richer. Deeper. More meaningful. The things we've experienced, the people we've become — none of it would have happened without the curse.

That's the terrible truth about being a vampire. It doesn't make you evil. It makes you eternal. And eternity gives you time to become anything you want.

Even good.
