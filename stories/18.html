The Forgotten Minute

2,400 words · 8 min read

There's a minute I can never remember.

It's not gone completely — I remember the before and the after. The morning was ordinary. Coffee, shower, the usual rush to get to work on time. And then I woke up in a hospital room, three days later, with no memory of what happened in between.

My wife says I collapsed. The doctors say it was a seizure. My daughter says she heard me scream.

But I don't remember any of it.

***

Memory is a strange thing. We think it's fixed — recorded, stored, preserved like books in a library. But it's not. It's fluid. Mutable. Every time we remember something, we change the memory. We add to it, subtract from it, reshape it to fit who we've become.

The minute I lost wasn't just time. It was identity. The gap in my life where I used to be.

***

They call it transient global amnesia. That's the medical term. But there's no term for what it feels like to lose yourself — even briefly — and not know who you were before.

I started keeping a journal after that. Every day, I write down what happened. Not just the facts — the feelings, the thoughts, the small moments that make up a life. Because I know now how easily it can all disappear.

***

My wife says I'm different since the incident. More present. More attentive. More focused on the small things.

"You used to be somewhere else," she told me once. "Even when you were here, you were somewhere else. Now you're here. Really here."

Maybe it took losing a minute to learn how to live in the rest.

***

There's a Buddhist concept called "beginner's mind." The idea that every moment is new. Every experience is fresh. We approach things with curiosity rather than assumption.

I think that's what the minute gave me. Not the minute itself — I can't remember it, so it gave me nothing — but the awareness that followed.

Every moment is a new beginning. Every breath is a fresh start. The past is gone, and the future isn't here yet. The only moment that exists is this one.

***

My daughter is twelve now. She has memories I don't share — inside jokes with her friends, moments at school, a whole life I don't know about. Sometimes I feel jealous. Sometimes I feel left out.

But then I remember: everyone has minutes they can't remember. Everyone has gaps. The important thing isn't filling them in. It's accepting that we're all incomplete.

We're all just trying to piece together who we are from fragments and guesses.

***

The minute is still gone. I don't expect it will ever come back. But that's okay.

Because in losing it, I found something more valuable than memory.

I found presence.

Here's what I know now: the past is a story we tell ourselves. The future is a dream we haven't had yet. The only real thing is this moment, right now, breathing in and breathing out.

And that's enough.

That's more than enough.
