They Walk Among Us

4,100 words · 14 min read

The first one I saw was on the subway.

I was coming home from work, exhausted, headphones in, ignoring the world. The car was crowded, which was unusual for that late hour. I looked up, annoyed, and that's when I saw her.

She looked human. That's what messed me up. Not a monster, not a green alien, not some gray-skinned creature from a movie. She looked like any other woman on the train — mid-thirties, business casual, reading something on her phone. The only thing wrong was her shadow.

It didn't move when she moved.

I blinked. Looked again. The shadow was there, perfectly normal, moving with her like it should. I laughed at myself. I was tired. I was seeing things.

But then I saw another one. And another.

***

My name is Zoe, and I'm a researcher. Not a crazy person — a researcher. I study social patterns, specifically how communities form and dissolve in urban environments. My job is literally to observe people. So when I started seeing them, I knew I wasn't imagining it.

They were everywhere.

I made a list. Started tracking. Noticed patterns. The first ones I called "The Suit" — always dressed professionally, always in business districts, always in groups of two or more. Then "The Students" — younger, backpack-wearing, hanging around universities. Then "The Old Ones" — elderly, in retirement communities, mostly invisible to everyone except other old people.

They never stood out. They never caused trouble. They just... were. Living among us, watching, waiting.

For what?

***

I started documenting everything. Photos, videos, location data. I was meticulous. Scientific. This was the most important research of my life.

But I made a mistake. I posted one video online.

The video was blurry — security camera footage from a coffee shop. It showed two of them sitting at a table, not drinking, not talking, just... watching. The video went viral. Millions of views. The comments were exactly what you'd expect: conspiracy theories, alien invasion predictions, jokes.

And then they found me.

***

It was 3 AM when someone knocked on my apartment door. Not knocking, really — more like a polite tap. I looked through the peephole and saw no one.

I opened the door anyway.

She looked like the woman from the subway. Same age, same general appearance. But her eyes... her eyes were different. Not human eyes. Too deep. Too old. Like looking into a well that had no bottom.

"You've seen us," she said. Not a question.

"Yes."

"You've been documenting us."

"Yes."

She nodded slowly. "Most people don't see. It's not that we're hiding — we've been here since before your cities were built. But humans have a remarkable capacity for not-seeing. You see what you expect to see."

"What are you?"

"We're watchers. We've always been watchers. We've watched your species grow from scattered bands of hunter-gatherers to the civilization you have now. We've watched wars and plagues and miracles. We've watched you reach for the stars and wonder about the meaning of it all."

"Why?"

She smiled. It was almost human.

"Because you're beautiful. Fragile and brief and brilliant. You burn so brightly and die so quickly. We don't understand it. We want to understand it. That's why we watch. That's why we've always watched."

"Are you going to hurt us? Invade? Take over?"

She laughed — a surprisingly warm sound.

We've had ten thousand years to do that. If we wanted to, we'd have done it already. We're not here to take. We're here to witness. Your species is approaching a threshold, and we want to see what happens next."

"What threshold?"

"That's not for me to say. But I'll give you a hint: it has to do with how you see yourselves. How you see each other. How you see us."

She turned to leave. Then stopped.

"You can keep documenting. We don't mind. But be careful — not everyone who sees us reacts well. And some of us..." She paused. "Some of us aren't as patient as I am."

"Wait. How do I contact you if I have questions?"

She looked back. "You'll see us. Just look."

And then she was gone.

***

I didn't post any more videos. But I kept watching. And I started to see them everywhere — not just in cities, but in small towns, in rural areas, in places no one would expect.

They were here before Columbus. Before Rome. Before Egypt. They watched the pyramids rise and the empires fall. They watched us discover fire, invent writing, split the atom. They watched us, and they never interfered.

But now they're preparing for something. That's what my research tells me. Something's coming — something we're going to do or become that they've been waiting ten thousand years to see.

And honestly? I'm excited.

Because they don't see us as a threat. They see us as fascinating. As beautiful. As worth watching.

That's the nicest thing anyone's ever said about us.

Even if they're not human.
