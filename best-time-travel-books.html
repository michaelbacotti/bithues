Best Time Travel Books — Across Every Era

Last updated: April 2026

Home › Books › Time Travel

Time travel is one of science fiction's most demanding subgenres — it requires the writer to either think rigorously about paradox and consequence or to make the formal structure of the novel do work that prose usually can't. The books on this list do one or both. These aren't stories where time travel is a gimmick; they're stories where time travel is the point.

Slaughterhouse-Five by Kurt Vonnegut

Billy Pilgrim becomes "unstuck in time" — he experiences his life in non-chronological order, from his WWII capture to his death, with trips to the planet Tralfamadorian in between. Vonnegut uses the fractured timeline to devastating effect: the bombing of Dresden is shown out of sequence so that the reader can't distance themselves from it with narrative comfort. It is formally time travel, but really it's about what it means to live with an event that can't be processed normally.

The Time Machine by H.G. Wells

The original. Wells invented the concept — a scientist travels eight hundred thousand years into the future and finds a dying Earth divided into two species, the悠闲 Eloi and the industrious Morlocks. The novel is short and the prose shows its age, but the central images are still powerful, and the social critique underneath the adventure is sharper than it appears at first reading.

Buy The Time Machine on Amazon →

The First Fifteen Lives of Harry August by Claire North

Harry August is born, lives, dies — and is reborn, retaining every memory. He is a "kalachakra," and there are rules about what kalachakras can and cannot do, about what happens when they try to change the past, and about the societies they've built across centuries. North (a pseudonym) writes with precision and restraint — the rules matter, the consequences are logical, and the emotional core of the book is about what infinite repetition does to a person.

Buy The First Fifteen Lives of Harry August on Amazon →

Dark Matter by Blake Crouch

A man is kidnapped and wakes up in a world where he's a different person — he has a different family, a different career, a different life. What follows is a thriller about the multiverse, constructed from a branching timeline model where every decision creates a new version of reality. Crouch writes propulsive action but the philosophical weight underneath is real: what does it mean to be the person you chose to be?

Buy Dark Matter on Amazon →

A Wrinkle in Time by Madeleine L'Engle

Three children travel through time and space using the "tesseract" — a folding of spacetime — to find their father, who has been missing while working on a mysterious project. L'Engle's novel is more fantastical than hard sci-fi, but it treats time as something that can be navigated, not just endured. The book's openness about good and evil, love and sacrifice, makes it resonate long after the science (or quasi-science) fades.

Buy A Wrinkle in Time on Amazon →

The Time Traveler's Wife by Audrey Niffenegger

Henry has a genetic condition that causes him to involuntarily jump through time — he arrives in his past or future with no warning and no control. His wife Clare has known him since she was six, even though she's only met him in the present at twenty-eight. Niffenegger uses the time structure not as a puzzle but as a metaphor for love that exists outside the linear — love that doesn't require simultaneity to be real.

Buy The Time Traveler's Wife on Amazon →

The Midnight Library by Matt Haig

Nora Seed has a night before she plans to end her life — but she gets to visit the lives she might have lived, each one corresponding to a different decision point. It's a premise that could be sentimental, but Haig keeps it honest: the alternate lives are not necessarily better, and the value of the book is in what it says about regret and choice without pretending there are easy answers.

Buy The Midnight Library on Amazon →

The Invisible Life of Addie LaRue by V.E. Schwab

Addie makes a Faustian bargain in 1714: she will live, but she will be forgotten by everyone she meets. She drifts through centuries, accumulating experiences but no connections — until she meets a man who remembers her. Schwab's novel spans three hundred years and uses the time-travel-adjacent structure to explore what it means to be present in a world that doesn't know you exist.

Buy The Invisible Life of Addie LaRue on Amazon →

Recursion by Blake Crouch

Crouch follows Dark Matter with a novel about memory — specifically, about what happens when a technology allows people to relive their lives from any point, erasing what came after. The book is about the societal consequences of that ability: what happens to crime, justice, identity, and mortality when you can go back and change the past? It's a thriller with a genuinely novel take on the mechanics of time manipulation.

Buy Recursion on Amazon →

An Unkindness of Ghosts by Rivers Solomon

A generation ship, centuries into its voyage, is organized like a antebellum plantation — with the same castes, the same violence, the same language of subservience. A biracial botanist discovers that the ship is off course and has been for years, and that the people in power have been covering it up. Solomon uses the sci-fi structure — a ship traveling through time as it travels through space — to examine how long-untended injustice compounds across generations.

Buy An Unkindness of Ghosts on Amazon →

💡 Key Takeaway

For formal innovation and literary depth, start with Slaughterhouse-Five or The First Fifteen Lives of Harry August. For propulsive reading that doesn't sacrifice ideas, try Dark Matter or Recursion. For something more lyrical and emotionally direct, The Time Traveler's Wife and The Midnight Library both reward the read.
